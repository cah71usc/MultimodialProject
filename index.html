The Problem - Wildfire Awareness

The Problem

Nearly 85% of all wildfires in the United States are caused by human activities. These include unattended campfires, burning debris, equipment use and malfunctions, discarded cigarettes, and intentional acts of arson.

Human-caused wildfires can occur at any time of the year, which is different from wildfires started by lightning that are more prevalent during the summer. These preventable fires threaten lives, destroy property, and consume valuable resources.

In recent years, wildfire seasons have grown longer and fires have become more severe due to climate change and increasing human presence in wildland areas.

Impact on Ecosystems

Wildfires significantly alter ecosystems. They burn away the forest floor, destroy animal habitats, and disrupt entire food chains. The intense heat and flames can eradicate native plant species and allow invasive species to take over, permanently changing the landscape.

Soil becomes vulnerable to erosion and less capable of holding water, which leads to increased flooding and landslides after rainfall. Rivers and lakes may become polluted with ash, affecting aquatic life.

How We Can Prevent Forest Fires

  • Never leave a campfire unattended: Always fully extinguish it with water, stir the ashes, and repeat until cool to the touch.
  • Dispose of cigarettes responsibly: Never throw cigarette butts on the ground or out of car windows, especially in dry areas.
  • Follow local fire bans: Be aware of fire restrictions in your area and comply with them strictly.
  • Avoid burning debris: Especially on windy or dry days. Consider composting or using community collection services instead.
  • Maintain equipment: Ensure vehicles, chainsaws, and other tools are in good condition and free of sparks or leaks.
  • Report suspicious activity: If you see someone being careless with fire, notify local authorities immediately.
  • Educate others: Share wildfire prevention tips with friends, family, and your community. Knowledge saves lives.
